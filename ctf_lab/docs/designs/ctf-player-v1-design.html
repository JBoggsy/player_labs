Capturing the Flag,
One Attributable Iteration at a Time

The strategic and tactical design for CTF’s first player policy — a deterministic reflex agent that reaches the enemy flag, shoots what its cone sees, and carries the win home.

Policy  CTF v1 · “minimal complete loop” Runtime  Python · Player-SDK SpriteV1 bridge Status  Design — pending approval Date  2026-07-10

CTF rewards exactly one thing: winning the round. A team earns +100 for a capture or a wipe and 0 for everything else — kills, deaths, and near-misses all score nothing. Every design choice below bends toward that single fact. We are not building a bot that fights well; we are building one that ends rounds in our favor, and fighting is only ever in service of that.

v1 is deliberately the smallest bot that plays a whole game end-to-end: it advances to the enemy flag, defends itself when an enemy enters its vision, steals the flag, and carries it home to score. It carries no roles, no enemy-tracking memory, and no exposure-aware routing yet — those are later, each added as one change measured against the last. The lab’s edge is iteration speed with attributable evals; a large v1 forfeits that on day one.

The objective is to end rounds in our favor — fighting is only ever in service of that. Design thesis

01The board, in one glance

Two teams of eight spawn on opposite edges of a fixed, cover-dense arena. Red holds the left; Blue holds the right. Each guards a flag on a home pedestal. There are two ways to win: carry the enemy flag into your own capture zone, or wipe every enemy life. No straight line crosses the field — every approach is a series of corners.

That last fact is load-bearing. Because the map never changes between episodes, the hardest part of navigation — turning a pixel-level wall mask into a routable graph — is done once, offline, and baked into the image. More on that in §4.

02How the agent thinks — one tick

The policy rides the SDK’s SpriteV1 bridge (run_sprite_bridge): the bridge owns the websocket and decodes each frame into a raw SpriteWorld; our code is pure game logic. Every frame flows through a five-stage pipeline — the same perceive → believe → decide → act shape proven by Heartleaf’s cady policy.

Perception never guesses geometry — it reads the game’s own labels: "self red right" locates our avatar (and its absence means we’re dead), "player blue left" marks a visible enemy, "aim dot red" reads our true aim back, "red flag" / "blue flag" report each flag’s state, and "fire icon" means the gun is ready. Our team and seat come for free from the connection slot: even slot = Red, odd = Blue; seat is slot ÷ 2.

03The one mechanic that governs everything: aim is vision

In CTF, the stick you aim with is also the eye you see with. Confirmed against the simulator’s fog-of-war code, a player sees exactly two regions and nothing else:

Two consequences drive v1’s tactics. First, rotating aim swings a long directional searchlight — where you point is both where you can shoot and, mostly, where you can see. Second, the gun has a ~0.2s windup during which aim locks: a target that ducks behind cover before the shot releases survives. So aim is a scarce resource, and v1 spends it deliberately.

The default-aim policy: a lighthouse sweep across the threat axis

When no enemy is visible, v1 does not merely hold the wedge still — it sweeps it like a lighthouse, panning back and forth across a 90° arc centered on the threat axis. The threat axis is a single, always-available direction: the unit vector from us toward the enemy pedestal, a static, never-fogged landmark. Because the pedestal sits deep in enemy territory, that one rule points the wedge forward into the enemy half while we advance, and — once we’ve grabbed the flag and turned for home — back across the field at our pursuers. No mode-switch; the geometry does the work.

The sweep is what makes it powerful. A still 90° wedge sees a fixed 90° slice; a wedge that pans ±45° across the axis drags its leading edge over a full 180° hemisphere facing the enemy every cycle — double the coverage, all of it where threats actually come from, with our back covered by the 90px bubble. One end-to-end pass takes about a second at the game’s turn rate, so it reads the field briskly rather than lazily.

The sweep yields the moment a threat appears: the engage overlay aborts the scan and snaps aim onto the enemy, fires when the shot-gate clears, then resumes sweeping. Because we sweep toward the enemy half, we usually detect a foe with the gun already roughly on them — worst-case extra rotation to line up the shot is under 45°, comparable to the fire windup itself. The one threat signal v1 leaves on the table is the sound ring a hidden shot emits; “aim where a shot just came from” is the natural v1.x refinement, deferred to keep v1 minimal.

Aim is scarce — it is the gun and the eye at once. v1 spends it as a lighthouse across the enemy axis, and snaps it to a target the instant one appears. Tactical principle · §3

04Navigation — solve it offline, stay flexible online

The arena defeats naive “hold the stick toward the flag”: cover is staggered so that heading straight for the pedestal walks you into a wall. Real routing is required. But routing has an expensive half (building the graph) and a cheap half (searching it) — and because the map is static, the expensive half happens once, offline.

This split honors the two requirements exactly: as much as possible is precomputed (the grid and the two strategic flow fields are solved once and shipped in the image, and can be eyeballed against the real map art before a single game runs), yet the agent keeps complete navigational freedom — chasing an enemy flag-carrier to an arbitrary point falls through to online A* on the very same grid. Nothing constrains movement to the fixed shrines.

05What v1 decides — the priority ladder

Each tick, the strategy stage picks one objective by walking a short priority ladder top to bottom and taking the first that applies. Combat is not a rung — it is an overlay that rides on top of whatever the agent is doing.

1

Carrying the enemy flag → run it home. Follow the “own capture zone” flow field to the score. Highest priority: a carried flag in hand is a win one delivery away, and dying drops it back to the enemy pedestal instantly.

2

Enemy flag on its pedestal → go steal it. Follow the “enemy pedestal” flow field to the flag and touch it. This is the default drive of every living agent that isn’t already carrying.

＋

Overlay · combat & sight. Regardless of the chosen objective: sweep aim as a lighthouse across the threat axis (§3); if an enemy is in the wedge or bubble, abort the sweep, snap aim onto it, and fire when the shot-gate clears; otherwise keep advancing and scanning. Movement and aim are decoupled, so the agent shoots without breaking stride.

That is the entire v1 brain: advance, defend yourself, steal, deliver. It produces a bot that reliably ends games — the platform on which every later capability is measured.

06Scope — what ships, what waits

The line between v1 and “later” is drawn to protect attribution: everything that ships is needed to complete a game; everything held back is a distinct capability we want to measure on its own.

In v1

  • Full game loop — advance, steal, carry, deliver
  • Self-defense combat — snap-aim & fire on visible enemies
  • Threat-axis aim — deliberate use of the vision wedge
  • Baked nav — grid + two strategic flow fields
  • Online A* — arbitrary-goal routing on the same grid
  • Diagnostics — CTF_DIAG per-tick + transition trace

Deferred to later iterations

  • Seat-based roles — rusher / flanker / defender split
  • Enemy-track memory — remember foes after they leave sight
  • Exposure-aware routing — avoid cells a foe can shoot
  • Carrier escort & hunt — coordinate around the flag
  • Peek-fire-duck micro — corner-fighting discipline
  • Team coordination — emergent, since there is no radio

Each deferred item becomes a future iteration: change one thing, rebuild, run a hosted eval, and read whether win rate moved. The Nim baseline bot already implements all of these — it is our reference opponent and our source of proven ideas, ported one at a time only once v1 tells us which gap costs us the most games.

07Where the pieces live

The policy is vendored under ctf_lab/<policy>/, mirroring Heartleaf’s cady. Each module owns one responsibility so behavior stays easy to trace and change.

ModuleResponsibility
main.pyEntry point — connect to the runner’s websocket, run the bridge (keepalive disabled: a slow frame must not drop the connection).
decide.pyBridge adapter + the CTF_DIAG diagnostic logger (per-tick snapshots and transition lines for post-mortems).
perception.pySprite-label lookups → a clean Observation (self, enemies, flags, aim readback, fire-ready).
belief.py · types.pyFold observations into Belief: team from slot, dead-reckoned aim estimate, flag states.
strategy.pyThe priority ladder — choose one objective per tick.
nav.py · mapdata.pyLoad the baked grid + flow fields; flow-field lookup or online A*; waypoint → octant.
tools/bake_map.pyOffline: build & verify the grid and flow fields; write the mapdata/ artifact.
action.py · config.pyIntent → 8-bit mask (move octant, aim rotation, fire-gate); tunable knobs isolated from logic.
